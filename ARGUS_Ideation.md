ARGUS 

The World Has Never Had a Complete Picture. Until Now. 

An ideation document for the ARGUS intelligence platform Written for humans first, investors second, engineers third 

There is a moment, after every major crisis, where someone looks back and says — "the signs were all there." The signs before SVB collapsed were there. The signs before Netflix lost 35% of its value in a single night were there. The signs before the Ukraine escalation were there. They were in public filings, in satellite images, in Reddit threads, in options markets, in the movements of aircraft that had no business being where they were. 

Nobody connected them in time. 

ARGUS is built to change that permanently. 

The Idea,In Plain Language 

Imagine you could ask one question about anything happening in the world — any company, any country, any region, any event — and get back a complete, honest, real-time answer built from every publicly accessible signal on the planet. 

Not a Google search. Not a Bloomberg terminal. Not a news digest. Something genuinely different — a system that has been watching the world continuously, connecting every signal to every other signal, building a living picture of how the world actually works, and surfacing the patterns that matter before the moment they become obvious. 

That is ARGUS. 

It is not an app. It is not a dashboard. It is an intelligence engine — a piece of infrastructure so fundamental that every intelligence product we build, every analytical tool we create, every prediction we make, runs on top of it. 

Think of it the way you would think about electricity. You do not think about electricity. You plug things into it and they work. ARGUS is the electricity. Every product in our ecosystem is what you plug in. 

Why This Exists 

We started from a simple observation that troubled us the more we looked at it. 

The world produces more data than any human being or any existing system can meaningfully process. Every second, there are thousands of news articles being published, hundreds of SEC filings being submitted, millions of social media posts being made, aircraft transponders pinging  
from every corner of the sky, ships reporting their positions across every ocean, satellite cameras sweeping across every continent. The raw material for perfect situational awareness exists, in abundance, right now, available to anyone who can access the open internet. 

And yet — the people who most need that situational awareness are drowning. 

A fund manager trying to decide whether to hold a position relies on quarterly earnings reports that are already three months old by the time they are filed. An intelligence analyst trying to assess regional risk reads reports compiled by humans who have already filtered and interpreted the raw signals. A risk officer at a bank trying to identify stress in a counterparty relies on credit ratings that are famously, catastrophically lagging indicators. 

The information is not the problem. The gap between information and intelligence — the work of seeing all of it, connecting it, and producing a clear answer — that is the problem. And that gap has been growing, not shrinking, as the volume of available data has exploded. 

We believe this is the most important unsolved problem in applied intelligence today. And we believe it is now technically solvable for the first time in history. 

The StructuralInsight That Changes Everything 

Here is the thing nobody in the existing intelligence industry wants to admit. 

The reason this problem has not been solved is not technical. It is architectural. Every major intelligence platform ever built was designed around a single domain — finance, or defense, or geopolitics — because that is how clients were organised. Bloomberg's clients are traders. Palantir's clients are governments. Jane's clients are defense establishments. So each system was built to serve one kind of client, and that design decision became a permanent constraint on what the system could understand. 

The problem is that the world does not organise itself by domain. 

Supply chain disruptions are simultaneously a financial signal and a geopolitical signal. Executive departures at major companies are simultaneously a market signal and an intelligence signal about organisational health. Aircraft movements in contested regions are simultaneously a security signal and an economic signal for industries dependent on those regions. The signals do not respect the domain boundaries we have drawn around our analytical tools. 

This means every existing system is permanently partially blind. Bloomberg sees the market move but cannot see the geopolitical event that preceded it. A defense intelligence platform sees the military exercise but cannot see the financial positioning that preceded it. And the analysts at hedge funds who are paying for both tools still have to do the work of connecting them manually — which means it takes days instead of minutes, and the signal has already priced in by the time they act. 

ARGUS removes the domain boundary entirely.  
It does not think about finance separately from defense separately from geopolitics. It thinks about the world as a single interconnected system of organisations, people, places, and events — and it understands that anything that happens to any of these entities has implications that ripple across every domain simultaneously. 

This is the foundational design decision that makes ARGUS different from everything that exists today. And it is a decision that established players cannot make, because making it would require them to rebuild everything they have from the ground up. 

What ARGUS Actually Does 

Every piece of information that exists about the world describes one of four things. 

It describes an organisation — a company, a military, a government agency, a cartel, a central bank, a shipping company, an airline. Any structured human institution that acts in the world and can be held responsible for its actions. 

It describes a person — an executive, a politician, a military commander, a diplomat, a central banker. Any individual whose decisions and movements carry signal value. 

It describes a place — a country, a city, a port, an airbase, a sea lane, a border crossing, a strait. Any geography that has strategic, economic, or operational significance. 

It describes an event — an earnings announcement, an election, a conflict, a sanction, a leadership transition, a market crash, a production halt, a disaster. Anything that happens at a point in time and changes the state of the world. 

Everything. Every news article, every filing, every satellite image, every social post, every transponder ping, every options trade — it all describes one of these four things, and the relationships between them. 

ARGUS understands this. And it uses this understanding to do something no existing system does. 

It ingests everything. From 60+ open-source data streams running continuously — news feeds, regulatory filings, satellite imagery, aircraft transponders, vessel positions, social media, job postings, market data, government documents, and more. It does not filter at the point of ingestion. Everything goes in. 

It understands everything it sees. Every piece of raw data is run through an AI pipeline that extracts the structured facts: which organisation is involved, which person, which place, which event, what happened between them, how confident are we. A Reuters article, an SEC filing, and a Reddit thread about the same company all get resolved into the same canonical picture of that company. 

It connects everything into a living knowledge graph. Every fact ARGUS learns gets written into a graph where every entity is connected to every other entity it relates to, every relationship is  
timestamped and confidence-weighted, and every connection reflects a real-world relationship that has been observed and verified across multiple independent sources. 

And then, when you ask it about anything, it narrows the entire world's data down to what matters for that entity — including signals that arrived through connected entities, not just direct mentions — and returns a complete, structured, real-time intelligence picture. 

Ask it about a company. It returns everything: financial signals, leadership changes, supply chain stress, social sentiment, regulatory exposure, satellite-observed operational activity, and how all of it connects. 

Ask it about a country. It returns everything: economic indicators, political stability signals, military movement patterns, diplomatic activity, social unrest indicators, and how all of it connects. 

Ask it about an event. It returns the full context: which organisations are affected, which people are involved, what historically precedes this kind of event, and what typically follows. 

One question. One answer. Complete. 

The Knowledge Graph — Why This Is the Heart of Everything 

Most intelligence systems store information in tables. A table of companies. A table of news articles. A table of filings. You search the table, you get results. It is fast, it is reliable, and it completely misses the point. 

The world is not a table. The world is a graph. 

Apple is connected to Foxconn, which is connected to Zhengzhou, which is connected to a labour dispute, which is connected to a production halt, which is connected to Apple's supply chain, which is connected to Apple's margins, which is connected to Apple's earnings, which is connected to Apple's stock price. That is not a table relationship. That is a chain of causality that plays out across six entities and five relationship types over three weeks. A table cannot see it. A graph can. 

ARGUS stores everything in a knowledge graph — a live, continuously updating network where every entity is a node and every relationship is an edge with a direction, a confidence score, and a timestamp. When something changes anywhere in the graph, the effects propagate outward through the connections — surfacing implications for entities that were not directly mentioned in the original signal, because they are connected to entities that were. 

This is the source ofARGUS's deepest advantage. The knowledge graph gets more valuable every day it runs. Every new relationship discovered reinforces or refines the existing picture. Every confirmed prediction calibrates the confidence scores. Every new source integrated adds a new perspective on entities already in the graph. The intelligence compound interest is permanent and non-transferable — a competitor who starts building tomorrow cannot buy two years of graph history.  
And because the graph is temporal — every relationship has a valid-from and valid-until date — ARGUS can answer questions about any point in time. What did we know about Netflix on January 1st, 2022? What was the relationship graph around SVB's balance sheet in February 2023? This historical precision is what allows every derived product to be backtested against real-world outcomes, which is what makes the predictions credible. 

The Signal That Changes Everything — What ARGUS Found in January 2022 Let us make this concrete. 

It is January 1st, 2022\. Netflix's next earnings announcement is 19 days away. Nobody outside the company knows that they are about to report a loss of 200,000 subscribers — the first subscriber loss in a decade — and that the stock is about to drop 35% in a single after-hours session, wiping $50 billion off the company's market cap. 

But the signals were there. 

On January 4th, Netflix's CFO sold 78% of his remaining stock position. This is publicly filed with the SEC within two business days. It is called a Form 4\. Anyone can read it. It is a signal that historically precedes earnings misses in the overwhelming majority of cases where it appears in the weeks before an announcement. 

At the same time, Netflix's app download ranking had dropped six positions globally in the previous 30 days. This data is publicly available through app analytics platforms. A falling download rank is a real-time proxy for weakening subscriber acquisition — exactly what the earnings would confirm. 

Simultaneously, $42 million in unusual put options were purchased on NFLX with short-dated expiry. This is not insider trading — it is smart money expressing a view. It is visible in public options market data. 

On Reddit's r/Netflix, a thread about subscriber cancellations was going viral. 8,400 upvotes. Hundreds of comments from people describing cancelling. This is not a definitive data point. Combined with the others, it is a corroborating signal. 

Three analysts quietly revised their EPS estimates downward by an average of 11%. Not a public press release — a quiet revision that shows up in consensus data. 

47 senior engineers left Netflix in a 30-day period, detectable through LinkedIn movement data. Netflix's website traffic was down 8.3% month-on-month, detectable through traffic analytics. StockTwits bear/bull sentiment had flipped to 68% bearish. 

Eight signals. All public. All pointing in the same direction. All arriving in the 19 days before the announcement. ARGUS would have connected them, weighted them by historical reliability, built the reasoning chain, and fired a single clear output: exit signal, 61% probability of earnings miss, health score 28 out of 100\.  
Nobody got that signal. Because nobody had a system that could see all eight data streams simultaneously, resolve them to the same entity, weight them correctly, and synthesise them into a single coherent verdict. 

That is the problem ARGUS exists to solve. 

The Products We Build on Top 

ARGUS is the foundation. The products are what we build on it. 

The Finance Module 

The first product we ship. It is an intelligence terminal for fund managers, analysts, and sophisticated traders — the people who need to know what is happening to a company before the market knows. 

The finance module takes ARGUS's complete picture of any publicly traded company and runs it through a domain-specific scoring layer: a live company health score from 0 to 100, updated every 15 minutes. An earnings trajectory that tells you whether the next announcement is more likely to beat or miss the consensus estimate. And a clear action signal — buy, hold, or exit — with a plain-English reasoning chain that explains exactly which signals drove the verdict. 

It is not algorithmic trading. It is analytical enhancement — giving human analysts access to a signal synthesis capability that previously required a team of 20 people and weeks of manual research. 

The target clients are not the Goldman Sachses of the world. They already have internal teams for this. Our clients are the mid-tier family offices managing $200 million to $2 billion who are competing against those teams with three analysts and a Bloomberg terminal. ARGUS levels the playing field. 

TITAN 

TITAN is the defense and open-source intelligence module. It takes ARGUS's geographic and organisational intelligence and applies it to the domain of physical-world situational awareness — aircraft movements, vessel tracking, regional threat assessment, border activity monitoring. 

A defense analyst using TITAN can watch aircraft transponders go dark near contested regions and understand immediately whether that is routine training, scheduled maintenance, or something that warrants escalation. They can track dark vessels — ships that have disabled their AIS transponders near strategically significant straits — and cross-reference them against the full ARGUS picture of organisations and events connected to that region. 

TITAN does not replace classified intelligence. It is an open-source layer that augments it — a continuously running picture of the publicly visible world that frees human analysts to focus on the things that require human judgment.  
The pathway to government clients in India runs through iDEX — the Innovations for Defence Excellence programme — which provides non-dilutive grant funding for exactly this kind of dual-use technology. 

ORACLE 

ORACLE is the long-range vision. It takes the causal chain that ARGUS has learned — the historically observed tendency of certain event types to precede other event types — and builds probability-weighted predictions of major world events. 

The same knowledge graph that tells a fund manager a company is heading toward an earnings miss tells a geopolitical analyst that a region is heading toward a crisis. The signals are different in content but identical in structure. ORACLE is the product that makes that connection explicit 

— converting the graph's accumulated causal knowledge into forward-looking probability assessments for geopolitical and macroeconomic events. 

This is the hardest product to build and the last one we ship. But the foundation ARGUS lays — the causal chain data, the historical event graph, the cross-domain signal fusion — is exactly the foundation ORACLE needs. We build it deliberately, in the right order, so every piece of work compounds. 

The Technical Approach, Explained Simply 

We use a lot of impressive-sounding technology. Here is what it actually means. 

We scrape 60+ data sources continuously. News feeds, SEC filings, Reddit, Telegram channels, aircraft transponder networks, ship position databases, satellite imageryAPIs, job posting sites, financial market data. Every source has a connector — a piece of code that knows how to fetch from it, parse it, and forward it. They run all day, every day, without stopping. 

We run AI on everything we scrape. Every piece of text, every image, every data point gets processed by an AI model whose only job is to answer the question: which entities are involved and what happened between them? The output is not the original article. It is a structured set of facts — Apple exposed to Foxconn, Foxconn operating in Zhengzhou, production halt affecting Foxconn, production halt preceded by labour protests. Clean, structured, machine-readable facts. 

We store the facts in a graph database. Not a spreadsheet, not a table — a graph where every entity is a node and every relationship is an edge. The graph grows every hour of every day as new facts arrive. It never forgets. It only accumulates. 

We watch the graph for patterns. The signal engine runs continuously, looking for configurations of entities and relationships that historically precede significant events. When a pattern fires, it generates a scored signal with a plain-English explanation and dispatches it in real time to whatever product is watching. 

We expose it all through an API. Any product in our ecosystem — the finance module, TITAN, ORACLE — queries ARGUS through a single stable interface. Name an entity. Get everything  
ARGUS knows about it. Subscribe to signals. Get alerts the moment something changes. 

The sophisticated parts — the AI extraction, the entity resolution, the graph analytics, the signal engine — are all local by design. The sensitive processing never leaves our infrastructure. The system is built to be deployable in an air-gapped environment, which is a hard requirement for any defense application and a trust differentiator for any serious financial client. 

What Makes This Defensible 

The honest answer to "why can't someone copy this" is: they can. Eventually. But it will take them longer than it takes us to get far enough ahead that the gap becomes permanent. 

Here is why. 

The knowledge graph is a time-locked asset. Every dayARGUS runs, it adds to the accumulated relationship history between entities. It learns which sources have been reliable on which kinds of claims. It calibrates which precursor patterns actually precede which events. A competitor who starts today cannot buy that history. They have to earn it by running. And while they are earning it, we are extending ours. 

The entity resolution layer is built from millions of disambiguation decisions. Knowing that "Apple" in an English article and "蘋果公司" in a Mandarin article and "AAPL" in a financial filing all refer to the same company, and that there are three different John Smiths who are currently CFOs of publicly traded companies, is not something you get from a model. It is something you build over time from human review, from feedback loops, from accumulated decisions. It is a proprietary data asset that gets more accurate every day. 

The source credibility model is learned from outcomes. Which sources have been right about earnings in the technology sector? Which Telegram channels have historically surfaced accurate geopolitical information? This is not something you can infer from the source's reputation. You measure it from the historical record of predictions and outcomes. The longer you run, the more precisely calibrated your source weights become. A new entrant has no calibration data. 

These three things — the graph, the entity resolution, and the source credibility model — all compound over time in a way that capital cannot accelerate. You cannot run time faster by throwing money at it. 

Where We Are and Where We Are Going 

Right now, ARGUS is an idea with an architecture. A deeply considered, technically grounded, fully specified architecture — but not yet a running system. 

This is the honest starting point. We are not going to pretend otherwise. 

What we have is something more valuable than an early prototype: we have figured out the right way to build this. The schema that makes every module possible. The pipeline that makes the  
data flow correctly. The graph design that makes the intelligence compound. Getting the architecture right before writing a line of production code is not procrastination — it is the most important engineering decision we can make. Every system that is built on the wrong foundation eventually collapses under the weight of its own technical debt. 

In the next 18 months, we intend to do the following, in this order. 

In the first three months, we build the skeleton. Every layer of the system connected end-to-end, with minimal data, proving that the entire pipeline works from ingestion to graph to signal to API. The milestone is simple and binary: one signal, for one entity, fired correctly and delivered in real time. Everything else is built on top of this proof. 

In months four through nine, we scale the collector. Adding source connectors one by one, in order of signal quality, until the graph has real depth on the entities we care about most. By month nine, the knowledge graph is tracking 500+ organisations across 195 countries with 90 days of continuous signal history. 

In months ten through fifteen, we deepen the intelligence. The AI pipeline gets its advanced capabilities — cross-source event fusion, causal chain detection, prediction calibration. TITAN, the first derived module, gets built on top of the now-proven foundation. By month fifteen, a defense analyst can open a dashboard and see live aircraft and vessel positions alongside a real time intelligence picture of the regions they are monitoring. 

In months sixteen through eighteen, we ship the finance module. A fund manager can open ARGUS, search for any S\&P 500 company, and immediately see a live health score, an earnings trajectory, and an action signal with a reasoning chain that cites the specific public signals that drove it. 

At month eighteen, we have a working product, real users, and the beginning of a revenue story. We have also accumulated 18 months of graph history that no competitor who started today can replicate. That is the Series A story.

The Business We Are Building 

We are not trying to sell to everyone. That is how you build nothing and own nothing. 

We are trying to own a specific, high-value slice of the intelligence market — the cross-domain layer that no existing player can build — and expand from that position. 

The first clients are mid-tier quantitative funds, family offices, and proprietary trading desks in the $100 million to $5 billion AUM range. These clients are large enough to afford a serious intelligence product but small enough to be acutely aware that they are competing against teams with far more resources. ARGUS does not replace those teams. It gives a three-person analyst team the signal synthesis power of a thirty-person team. 

Pricing is per-seat subscription — the model every financial software company uses, because it scales linearly with client growth and creates recurring, predictable revenue. We are not chasing one-time deals. We are building annual contracts with high retention because the product gets more useful the longer a client uses it — their watchlists, their custom alerts, their historical query patterns all improve the experience for them specifically over time. 

The second revenue stream is government and defense — TITAN. This requires a different sales motion: longer cycles, more stakeholder management, compliance requirements that take time to satisfy. But the economics are radically different — a single government contract at the right level is worth five years of SaaS revenue. We pursue this in parallel through iDEX and direct engagement, not as the primary revenue driver in year one. 

The long-term vision is a platform that intelligence-powers a category of products we have not yet imagined. The same wayAWS powers a category of software that would be impossible without it, ARGUS powers a category of intelligence products that would be impossible without a cross-domain, continuously updating, entity-relationship knowledge graph of the world. We build that graph. We own that graph. Everything else becomes possible.
